## Executive Summary: The "Why" and "What"

Gaps in stock prices occur when there is a notable difference between the closing price of one day and the opening price on the next day, without any trading occurring between these two prices. This can happen due to several factors such as earnings announcements, other market news, or overall economic shifts. 

Gap fills happen when the price moves back to the original pre-gap level. This often occurs because prices tend to return to a state of equilibrium; the gap may represent an overreaction to news or events. The strategy revolving around this concept is known as **Gap Fill Trading**, a popular approach due to its straightforward identification and clear target levels.

Understanding and leveraging gap fill strategies can significantly enhance a trader's repertoire, particularly in volatile markets. These strategies provide clear parameters for trades, potentially offering high rewards relative to risks.

## The Institutional Perspective: How Banks/Algos View This vs How Retail Views It

**Institutional traders**, such as those at banks or large fund houses, often view gap trades as opportunities to exploit inefficiencies in pricing created by overreactions from retail traders. Institutions use advanced algorithms to quickly identify and act upon these opportunities, often filling the gaps faster than retail traders can.

**Retail traders**, on the other hand, generally react to gaps with a mix of excitement and trepidation, driven mostly by fundamental news or large movements seen in the stock's price. Their entries might be based less on systematic approaches and more on emotional reactions or a simplified analysis.
  
## Core Mechanics: Deep Dive into the Theory

### Analogies to Understand Gap Filling
Imagine a gap in trading as a sudden break in a bridge. Vehicles (traders) must find a way back to the main path, that hole (the gap) ultimately needs to be filled for traffic to normalize.

### Why Gaps Fill
1. **Price Memory**: Markets tend to remember recent prices where transactions were previously agreed upon. A quick move away from these levels often warrants a retest.
2. **Liquidity and Overreaction**: Extreme reactions to news often create gaps. As emotions cool, traders looking for value drive the price back to fill the gap.
3. **Market Maker Behavior**: Market makers aim to maintain an orderly market, and in doing so, they might help steer prices to fill the gaps.

## Strategy & Execution: Step-by-Step Setup

### Identifying Potential Gap Fills
* Look for stocks that have gapped significantly due to news events or other market drivers.
* Ensure there's no additional news post-gap that might justify the new price level.

### Entry, Stop Loss, Take Profit
1. **Entry**: Enter a trade with a target to fill the gapâ€”the point where the price was before the gap occurred.
2. **Stop Loss**: Place a stop loss just outside the gap boundary, considering a buffer for volatility (about 1-2% of the stock price).
3. **Take Profit**: Set the take profit just before the gap fills completely, to account for any reversal just before the original gap price.

### Example
Imagine stock XYZ closed at $100 yesterday and opened today at $90, with no news justifying the $10 drop.

- **Entry**: $90
- **Stop Loss**: $88 (slightly more than 2% lower)
- **Take Profit**: $99

## Common Pitfalls: Where Most Traders Lose Money with This

**1. Chasing Gaps Indiscriminately**: Not all gaps are equal. Blindly trading every gap without assessing the reason behind them often leads to losses.

**2. Ignoring Market Context**: Gaps in a strong bullish or bearish trend might not fill as quickly as those in a range-bound market.

**3. Poor Risk Management**: Risking too much on a single gap trade can be detrimental. The key to consistency lies in balanced risk management.
